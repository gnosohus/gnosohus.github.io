Making insurance
feel like something
you'd actually use.

As the founding product designer at Thimble, I shaped the entire customer-facing experience from a blank canvas — turning small business insurance, an industry famous for fine print and friction, into something a contractor could buy from their phone in under five minutes. Six years, two platforms, one acquisition.

01 — The Problem

Insurance was built for insurers,
not for the people buying it.

Photographers, contractors, dog walkers, food truck owners — millions of small operators needed liability coverage to get hired, and the existing options were the same as they'd been for decades: phone calls, brokers, paperwork, week-long waits. Our challenge was to compress all of that into a self-serve flow that someone could complete between gigs, without ever speaking to a person.

"I just need to show this venue I'm covered by Saturday."
— Small business owner, user interview · 2019
Mobile app — quote flow, key screens stacked vertically
Mobile app — purchase confirmation, certificate, dashboard
02 — Design Framework

A foundation built
once, used everywhere.

As founding designer on a team of seven, I owned the full design framework: the transactional mobile app, the marketing site, the visual identity, even the deck that closed our Series A. Working directly with the CEO and early stakeholders, I helped translate a new SaaS model for episodic liability coverage into something users could actually grasp. Every surface had to feel like the same product. I built the early system around three principles — plain language over jargon, momentum over completeness, and one decision per screen — and applied them consistently across mobile, web, and email.

Web app — quote and checkout flow
Brand system — logo, typography, color application
Marketing site — homepage and product landing pages, full width
Post-purchase dashboard — personalized home view
Cancellation flow — alternative options (pause, downgrade, rewards)
Mobile screens — pause coverage, manage policy, help center, notifications
03 — Scaling the Story

When growth shifted,
the design had to follow.

In the first two years we obsessed over acquisition. As subscriptions matured, the question changed: how do we keep customers between gigs, between seasons, between policies? Cancellation surveys and traffic analysis revealed two patterns — users who left because the product felt rigid, and involuntary churn from failed payments. I led an end-to-end revamp: a personalized dashboard with "what's next" prompts, a pause option instead of a hard cancel, a rewards program for referrals, backup payment methods, and an in-app help center. A one-time transaction became an ongoing relationship — and Fast Company named it a finalist for Best UX Design of 2021.

Design system — atomic components, color tokens, type scale
Figma library — component variants, documentation pages
04 — Behind the scenes

A design system, rebuilt
for a remote team.

When the team went fully remote during the pandemic, I led the migration from Sketch to Figma and rebuilt the design system from atomic principles. Tokens, components, and patterns were all versioned and shared — letting a small team ship product faster, with more consistency, across more surfaces than its headcount should have allowed.

Designing for the people

Year

A new question for Thimble: how do you design a product not for the end customer, but for the agent placing coverage on their behalf? I led the end-to-end UX of the broker platform — onboarding, purchase, and portal — opening a D2B2C channel that grew Gross Written Premium by 900% in two years and helped lead Thimble to its acquisition by Arch Insurance in April 2023.

Context image — broker workflow before Thimble (spreadsheets, emails, multi-carrier comparison). 16:9
Market data visualization — D2C vs broker channel sizing
Broker interview photos / workflow audit
01 — The Opportunity

A new channel,
a new kind of user.

During the pandemic, Thimble's flexible, on-demand coverage gained traction in places we hadn't designed for — brokers placing policies for clients who couldn't get covered fast enough through traditional carriers. Together with PMs, customer support, insurance, and BD, I ran broker interviews and webinars to understand how they actually work: dozens of clients in motion, multi-carrier comparison spreadsheets, fifteen-minute phone calls that needed a quote on the screen by minute three. The opportunity was clear — a D2B2C channel that could meaningfully expand revenue without diluting the consumer product. But brokers don't behave like consumers, and pretending otherwise would have killed the channel before it started.

"Brokers don't want to be charmed.
They want to be fast."
— Insight from broker discovery interviews
Broker onboarding — signup, license verification, agency setup
Broker dashboard — clients, policies, renewals at a glance
02 — A Different User

Built for volume,
not for a single sale.

A consumer buys insurance once or twice a year. A broker writes dozens of policies a week, across multiple clients, often inside a fifteen-minute phone call. The design language had to shift: dense information over generous whitespace, keyboard shortcuts over decorative buttons, repeatable flows over discovery moments. I rebuilt the system to support both audiences from the same foundation — same brand, two very different paces.

Quote and bind flow — broker view (multi-client)
Policy management — endorse, renew, cancel in bulk
Enterprise tier — agency-level admin, team permissions, reporting. Full width screenshot.
Certificate of insurance generator — instant download / email
Mobile companion — on-the-go quote and bind for field brokers
03 — The Product

One platform.
From a solo agent to an agency of 200.

The platform scaled from a single broker placing one policy to enterprise agencies running entire teams — with role-based permissions, agency-level reporting, and white-glove onboarding for the largest accounts. An "appetite checker" tool up front — four inputs: location, business type, coverage, duration — let brokers know in seconds whether Thimble could write the risk, eliminating the dead-end quotes that erode trust with broker users. Every flow was designed to disappear: a broker shouldn't have to think about the tool, only about the client on the other end of the line.
